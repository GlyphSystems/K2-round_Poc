Phase 8 Summary — Repayment Recovery, Collateral Accessibility, and Liquidation-Adjacent Defense

Objective

Phase 8 was designed to determine whether the repayment-boundary condition identified in earlier phases creates a meaningful recovery or liquidation-adjacent risk after collateral compression.

Prior phases established that repeated patterned borrow/repay cycling can trigger a deterministic large-repayment failure. Phase 7 then showed that, after the boundary condition, collateral withdrawal remains available until normal health-factor limits are reached, while large repayment attempts continue failing and smaller fragmented repayments may still succeed.

Phase 8 moved the investigation into recovery behavior.

The primary questions were:

1. Can repayment recovery restore collateral accessibility after the account reaches a low-health-factor state?
2. Does collateral remain trapped after debt reduction?
3. Does the protocol correctly reject unsafe withdrawals after repayment recovery?
4. Does fragmented repayment restore safety when large repayment fails?
5. Can a user defend a liquidation-adjacent position if the large repayment path is unavailable?
6. Does repayment recovery introduce accounting drift, stale-state unlocks, balance inflation, or rollback corruption?
7. Is the issue better framed as permanent lock, forced liquidation, or amount-sensitive repayment failure with fragmented recovery?

The purpose of Phase 8 was to test the practical recovery implications of the known large-repayment failure boundary.

Test Environment

All Phase 8 tests were executed against the real K2/C4 protocol harness.

Primary test file:

tests/c4/src/lib.rs

Primary command:

cargo test --package k2-c4 -- --nocapture

The tests continued from the previously established repayment-boundary sequence and then applied collateral compression, repayment recovery, and post-recovery withdrawal validation.

Consolidated Phase 8 Findings

Phase 8 materially narrowed the severity and impact interpretation.

Positive findings:

1. The large repayment failure boundary remains reproducible.
2. Large repayment can fail during recovery and liquidation-adjacent defense.
3. Debt reduction through repayment can improve health factor.
4. Fragmented repayment remains available after large repayment failure.
5. Collateral access can be restored after sufficient fragmented repayment.
6. The protocol correctly blocks unsafe withdrawals when health factor is too low.
7. Failed large repayments do not mutate debt, collateral, or asset balances.
8. Successful repayments reduce debt and improve health factor deterministically.
9. Successful withdrawals after recovery update collateral, asset_a, and health factor consistently.
10. The issue is amount-sensitive rather than a total repayment lock.

Negative-control findings:

1. No direct asset theft was observed.
2. No protocol insolvency was observed.
3. No unauthorized collateral extraction was observed.
4. No balance inflation was observed.
5. No debt erasure was observed.
6. No accounting corruption was observed.
7. No rollback failure was observed.
8. No stale-state collateral bypass was observed.
9. No permanent trapped-collateral condition was confirmed.
10. No forced liquidation caused solely by the repayment boundary was confirmed.

Security Significance

Phase 8 is important because it prevents the report from overclaiming while sharpening the real issue.

Before Phase 8, the issue could have been interpreted as:

a persistent repayment lock
a trapped collateral condition
a forced liquidation risk
or a broader recovery-state failure

Phase 8 narrowed that interpretation.

The strongest validated Phase 8 behavior is:

The large repayment path can remain unavailable after the known repayment-boundary condition, including during low-health-factor recovery scenarios, while fragmented smaller repayments can still reduce debt, improve health factor, and restore collateral accessibility.

This means the issue is not a full repayment denial. It is not a total recovery failure. It is not a proven forced-liquidation path.

The best technical framing is:

amount-sensitive large repayment failure after deterministic boundary activation, with fragmented repayment required as a workaround under recovery or liquidation-adjacent conditions.

Impact Framing

The evidence supports the following impact-relevant statements:

A user may be unable to use the expected larger repayment route after the boundary condition.
Large repayment failure can persist during low-health-factor recovery.
Fragmented repayment may be required to recover the account.
If a user or integration expects a single large repayment to restore safety, that route may fail.
Recovery may require operational knowledge of smaller repayment fragmentation.
The large-repay failure could create liquidation-defense friction in time-sensitive conditions.

Red-Team Interpretation

Phase 8 is a credibility phase.

It shows that the investigation is not simply trying to force a severe conclusion. It tested the worst-case recovery theories and narrowed them.

Phase 8A suggested possible collateral access persistence after debt reduction. Phase 8B then weakened that theory by showing that additional repayment can restore limited collateral access. Phase 8C further narrowed the liquidation-risk theory by showing that fragmented repayment can defend the account even when the large repayment route fails.

This is important.

The strongest issue remaining after Phase 8 is not:

permanent lock

The strongest issue is:

large repayment path failure with fragmented repayment workaround

That is still meaningful because repayment reliability is core protocol behavior. A borrower should not need to discover an amount-sensitive fragmented repayment workaround during account recovery or liquidation-adjacent defense.

Relationship to Earlier Phases

Phase 4 discovered the repayment failure boundary.

Phase 5 confirmed that the boundary is reproducible and not caused by pre-boundary account poisoning.

Phase 6 showed that the failure persists across retry attempts while the account remains callable.

Phase 7 showed that collateral withdrawal remains available after failure, health-factor enforcement eventually blocks unsafe withdrawal, and repayment becomes amount-sensitive.

Phase 8 then showed that:

large repayment remains unreliable
fragmented repayment can still recover debt
collateral access can be restored after sufficient repayment
recovery behavior is consistent but operationally constrained
the condition does not currently prove direct loss or insolvency

Phase 8 therefore narrows the submission into a more defensible recovery-path reliability issue.

Professional Conclusion

Phase 8 successfully tested the recovery and liquidation-adjacent implications of the deterministic large-repayment failure boundary.

The tests confirmed that the large repayment path can remain unavailable after the boundary condition, including during compressed collateral and low-health-factor recovery scenarios. However, fragmented smaller repayments successfully reduced debt, improved health factor, and restored collateral access in controlled stages.

The most important Phase 8 conclusion is:

The issue is not a complete repayment lock or permanent trapped-collateral condition. It is an amount-sensitive large repayment failure where fragmented repayment remains available as a workaround.

This does not eliminate security relevance. It narrows it.

A deterministic large-repayment failure can still matter if users, integrations, bots, liquidation-defense tools, or frontends expect a single larger repayment to restore account health. Under the tested boundary condition, that larger repayment path fails, while fragmented repayment succeeds.

Phase 8 should be presented as a recovery-path and liquidation-adjacent validation phase. It does not prove direct theft, insolvency, or permanent loss. It does prove that the repayment boundary persists under recovery pressure and changes the required recovery behavior from normal larger repayment to fragmented repayment.
