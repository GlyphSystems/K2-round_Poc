Phase 7 Summary — Post-Failure Collateral Exit, Repayment Asymmetry, and Rollback Integrity

Objective

Phase 7 was designed to determine whether the persistent repayment failure identified in Phase 6 creates meaningful post-failure impact during continued protocol interaction.

Earlier phases established that repeated patterned residual borrow/repay cycling can deterministically trigger a Cycle 10 repayment failure, producing UnderlyingTransferFailed while visible asset_b balance remains present. Phase 6 further showed that the failure can persist after retry attempts and that the account remains callable after the repayment boundary is reached.

Phase 7 moved from persistence validation into impact-path testing.

The primary questions were:

1. Can collateral still be withdrawn after the repayment path enters the failure state?
2. Does debt remain active while collateral exits?
3. Does the protocol enforce health-factor limits during post-failure withdrawals?
4. Does repayment behavior become amount-sensitive after the boundary?
5. Can fragmented repayments reduce debt when larger repayments fail?
6. Do failed repay, borrow, and withdraw operations mutate state unexpectedly?
7. Does repeated failed execution create hidden accounting drift?
8. Does low-health-factor enforcement remain intact after the repayment boundary?
9. Does stale state propagate across mixed operation sequences?

The purpose of Phase 7 was to determine whether the repayment failure remains isolated to repayment execution, or whether it creates a broader protocol-state risk involving collateral exit, recovery behavior, withdrawal limits, failed-operation rollback, or liquidation-adjacent exposure.

Security Significance

Phase 7 is significant because it moves the investigation beyond repayment failure persistence and into post-failure account behavior.

The strongest validated Phase 7 behavior is:

After deterministic repayment-boundary failure, the account can continue withdrawing collateral while active debt remains present and larger repayment remains unavailable, until normal health-factor enforcement eventually blocks further collateral exit.

That is the central Phase 7 finding.

The second major finding is:

After the boundary condition, repayment becomes amount-sensitive. Smaller or fragmented repayments can reduce debt, while larger repayment attempts continue failing with UnderlyingTransferFailed.

Together, these findings support a credible post-failure recovery-risk argument:

repayment failure is persistent
large repayment remains unavailable
collateral exit remains available
health factor can degrade
fragmented repayment may be required to recover
failed operations roll back correctly
protocol accounting remains mostly internally consistent under tested paths

This is not a clean “everything breaks” result. It is more precise:

The protocol preserves many invariants, but the repayment path enters a deterministic amount-sensitive failure mode that changes user recovery behavior and allows collateral reduction while debt remains active.

Professional Conclusion

Phase 7 successfully converted the post-failure repayment instability from Phase 6 into a more complete post-boundary impact analysis.

The tests showed that after the known repayment failure boundary is triggered, the account remains callable, collateral withdrawal remains available, and active debt persists. Substantial collateral can be withdrawn while health factor declines, and normal health-factor enforcement eventually blocks further withdrawal. Larger repayment attempts continue failing with UnderlyingTransferFailed, while smaller or fragmented repayments can still reduce debt.

The most important Phase 7 conclusion is:

The repayment failure is not an isolated failed call. It creates a persistent post-boundary state where larger repayment remains unavailable, collateral withdrawal can continue under normal health-factor limits, and repayment recovery becomes amount-sensitive.

Phase 7 does not prove direct asset theft, insolvency, full health-factor bypass, or failed-operation corruption. It does establish a serious and reproducible post-failure behavior pattern that may be relevant to repayment reliability, user recovery, liquidation defense, and protocol-state consistency under boundary conditions.

This phase should be presented as a high-value impact-validation phase that strengthens the submission through both positive findings and negative controls.
